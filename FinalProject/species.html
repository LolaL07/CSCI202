Although this is a guide for intertidal species found in Washington state, the majority of these can be found along the entire west coast of North America from California to Alaska. Also, this should be used as a starting point rather than an all encompassing guide. You will certainly find a species not listed here, at some point, and I would encourage the use of further outside research to help aid your quest. iNaturalist is very helpful for identifying and recording observations.

Cnidaria

Cnidaria is the phylum that includes jellyfish, sea anemones, and corals. Many of the creatures within this category have tentacles with stinging cells called nematocysts.

Jellyfish

Moon Jelly | Aurelia aurita

Moon jellies have clear purple, blue, or white bodies with an opaque four leaf clover shape on the top of their bell. The can be up to about 15 inches wide and lack the long stinging tentacles that many others have.

Egg Yolk Jelly | Phacellophora camtschatica

Egg yolk jellies, or fried egg jellies, have transluscent white bodies with and pale yellowish center and long white tentacles. Their bell can grow to be up to 18 inches wide.

Lion's Mane Jelly | Cyanea capillata

Lion's mane jellyfish are a very large species of jellyfish and are known for their thick and long "mane" of tentacles. Their bell can grow up to 20 inches wide and their tentacles over 6 feet long. They have a very strong sting and should not be touched.

Anemones

Giant Plumose Anemone | Metridium farcimen

These unique anemones can be distinguished by their tall stalk and feathery tentacles on top. The usually come in orange or white and will grow up to 3 feet tall

Aggregating Anemone | Anthopleura elegantissima

Aggregating anemones are small anemones that grow up to about 2 inches wide. They are often found in groups or colonies and are greenish in color.

Painted Anemone | Urticina crassicornis

Painted Anemones come in various colors, mostly red, pink, brown, green, or olive, with blotches or stripes of the same colors. Because of their coloring, they are also sometimes called Christmas anemones. Their tentacles will also be striped or banded with red or white

Green Surf Anemone | Anthopleura xanthogrammica

These are large entirely green anemones that get their color from symbiotic photosynthesizing zooxanthellae and zoochlorollae that live in it. They are usually found on exposed rocky shores along the coasts and will grow up to 12 inches wide and tall. These creatures are also called giant green anemones.

Crabs

Green Shore Crab | Hemigrapsus oregonensis

Despite their name, green shore crabs can come in a range of colors including, green, yellow, brown, purple, or gray. They can grow up to about 2 inches wides and look very similar to purple shore crabs. Their main distinguishing feature is the small hairs or spines on the underside of their legs. They are also called yellow shore crabs.

Purple Shore Crab | Hemigrapsus nudus

Like the green shore crabs, they can come in a range of colors including, green, yellow, brown, purple, or gray. They can grow up to about 2 inches wides. Their main distinguishing feature from green shore crabs is the lack of small hairs or spines on the underside of their legs.

Red Rock Crab | Hemigrapsus oregonensis

Despite their name, green shore crabs can come in a range of colors including, green, yellow, brown, purple, or gray. They can grow up to about 2 inches wides and look very similar to purple shore crabs. Their main distinguishing feature is the small hairs or spines on the underside of their legs.

Northern Kelp Crab | Pugettia producta

Unlike others, these kelp crabs are known for their shield-shaped shell, which is where they get their other name, shield-backed crab. Their color ranges from green to brown to yellow to red. Additionally, they have much longer and thinner legs and and claws

Echinoderms

The animals within the echinoderm phylum are sea cucumbers, sea stars, and sea urchins. The term 'echinoderm' comes from greek roots that mean 'spiny skin'

Sea Cucumbers

California Sea Cucumber | Apostichopus californicus

These creatures are known for their deep red skin and large rounded orange spikes randomly scatted around their body. They have soft cylindrical bodies that can grow up to 16 inches long. These sea cucumbers are the largest species of their kind along the coast of the pacific northwest.

Sea Stars

Ochre Star | Pisaster ochraceus

These sea stars can come in a range of colors, including orange, purple, red, and brown. They have a larger central disk and short wide arms

Mottled Star | Evasterias troschelii

These sea stars can come in a range of colors, including orange, purple, red, brown, and blue-grey. They have a smaller central disk and long thin arms. They also have lines of white dots along their arms.

Leather Star | Dermasterias imbricata

Leather stars are known for their soft smooth skin. They are usualy a greyish blue color with reddish brown mottling

Sunflower Star | Pycnopodia helianthoides

Sunflowers stars are easily distinguished by their many arms, more than the normal five. They can have up to 24 and will be orange, purple, or reddish brown in color, with the underside being paler than the top. Their central disk can grown up to 16 inches wide

Sea Urchins

Green Sea Urchin | Strongylocentrotus droebachiensis

Green sea urchins have a light greenish body with radially symmetric dark purple or brown stripes. They have short fine spines that are about an inch long

Red Sea Urchin | Mesocentrotus franciscanus

Red sea urchins are known for their long spines that grow up to 2.5 inches long and their dark red or purple. They also grow larger than green and purple sea urchins usually do.

Purple Sea Urchin | Strongylocentrotus purpuratus

Purple sea urchins are quite similar to green sea urchins with short spines, radial symmetry, and dark tube feet. Their main distinguishing feature is their dark purple colored body and spines.
